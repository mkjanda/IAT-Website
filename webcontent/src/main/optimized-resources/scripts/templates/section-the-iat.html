The Implicit Association Test (IAT) offers two important tools. One is that it offers a test administrator the capacity to measure attitudes and biases that a subject is not willing to admit. Perhaps more interesting is the second tool. The IAT offers the test administrator the ability to measure attitudes and biases of which the test taker might not be aware.

Beyond determining whether John or Mary is a pedophile or a bigot, it can be used to measure thought processes on an unconscious level. It can determine how people think or feel about things in an intuitive way that doesn't involve rational thought. These invisible thought processes have been shown to predict or account for phenomena in ways that other measurements cannot.

So how does it work? The concept is simple when grasped but not the easiest to explain. An IAT measures bias between pairings of words or images. For instance, suppose you wanted to find out if John is a bigot. You might try to determine whether he associates the word Black with the word Good or the word Bad.

Here you see a picture of Dr. Martin Luther King Jr with two response keys. The subject is asked to categorize this picture as being either of a black person or a white person by selecting Black or White.

Here you see a broken heart that is to be placed in the category in the upper-right corner as "Bad." This is the cornerstone of IAT design, to devise two concepts such as "black" and "white" and then two words that lie at polar opposite ends of the same continuum. This way, you are able to determine which end of the continuum the subject associates each concept with.

Each IAT is divided into seven blocks. The first two blocks supply a response key that contains only the concept words or only the continuum words. For instance, the first block might have the response value Black in the upper-left corner and White in the upper-right corner. The subject would be shown faces of black and white people and asked to categorize them by pressing keys on their keyboard. Then the second block would have Good in the upper-left corner and Bad in the upper-right corner. For this block, the subject would see stimuli – words or images – that are categorically good or bad.

Blocks three and four appear identical to the test taker and are only regarded as separate for the purpose of scoring the test. In these blocks, the two response keys from blocks one and two are combined to form the response keys you saw on the previous screen. During these blocks, the subject would be shown pictures of white and black people as well as stimuli that are categorically good or bad. The idea is that if the subject associates Good with Black, he will provide correct responses faster for those stimuli.

Block five is largely a rehash of block two, with the response keys transposed from left to right. Blocks six and seven consist of the combined response keys as did blocks three and four, however the pairings are reversed, so Black would appear with Bad and White would appear with Good. This provides a counter-point to the measurements made in blocks three and four.
